# Crisis Recovery

**Regime ID:** crisis-recovery
**Document version:** 0.1 (draft)
**Date:** 2026-05-29
**Owner:** Mike White
**Status:** Draft

## Identification

A crisis recovery is the phase that follows a sharp risk-off shock — the months after the bottom is in, when the index is rallying off a panic low, breadth thrust signals fire, credit spreads compress aggressively, and the dominant trade is "risk-on, every dip is a buy." The macro picture is still grim by historical standards (unemployment elevated, earnings depressed) but is improving from a worst-point. The regime is distinct from "balanced expansion" because the rally is fueled by mean-reversion from extreme oversold conditions and by massive policy response, not by underlying fundamental strength.

## Statistical Markers

| Marker | Expected range | Notes |

| Implied vol (VIX) | 25-40 early, declining toward 18-25 | Term structure normalizes from inversion |
| VIX term structure | Backwardated at start; flattening then contango within months | The flip is diagnostic |
| Trend strength (SPX ADX 14) | Strong and rising (30+) | Persistent up-move off the low |

| Sector dispersion | High initially (cyclicals lead), normalizing later | Cyclical leadership is the tell |
| Credit spreads | Wide at start (HY 800+ bps), compressing fast | Spread compression confirms regime |
| Dollar trend | Often weakening (risk-on flows out of safety) | Variable |
| Rate vol (MOVE) | Declining from crisis spike | Policy clarity restoring |

## Historical Examples

- **2009-03 to 2009-12 (post-GFC):** SPX bottomed 2009-03-09, rallied ~65% by year-end. Breadth thrust in 2009-03 (Zweig thrust fired), credit spread compression dramatic, low-quality leadership characteristic. The fundamental backdrop remained grim (10%+ unemployment) but improving from a worse point.
- **2020-04 to 2020-08 (post-COVID-crash):** SPX bottomed 2020-03-23, rallied ~50% by August. Massive fiscal and monetary response, breadth thrust, credit spread compression. Distinctive feature: speed (recovery measured in months, not years) due to scale of policy response and idiosyncratic nature of shock.

## Macro Backdrop

Crisis recoveries share a common qualitative shape:

- A recent panic low driven by an identifiable shock (financial crisis, pandemic, exogenous geopolitical event).
- Aggressive policy response: rate cuts, quantitative easing, fiscal stimulus, regulatory forbearance.
- Improving but still-poor fundamentals: unemployment elevated, earnings depressed, capacity utilization low.
- A dominant "the worst is behind us" narrative competing with persistent fears of a re-test.
- Credit markets functioning again after a period of dislocation.
- Cyclicals and high-beta lead; defensives lag in relative terms.

The regime is distinct from "the dead-cat bounce" (which fails) by the breadth and persistence of participation, by the durability of credit-spread compression, and by the policy response being commensurate with the shock.

## What Works

- **Long-beta exposure during the early phase:** High-beta names, small caps, cyclicals tend to lead by wide margins. Trend-following on these works.
- **Credit-spread compression trades:** HY and IG spread compression is a persistent tailwind for credit-sensitive equities.
- **Buy-the-dip on the index:** This is the rare regime where the textbook "buy the dip" tactic actually has positive expected value because the trend is persistent and pullbacks are shallow.
- **Volatility-selling carry:** Realized vol falls faster than implied; vol-selling structures collect a persistent premium.
- **Long-call structures on cyclicals:** Asymmetric payoff when leadership extends.

## What Fails

- **Shorting the rally on "valuation":** Multiples expand throughout the regime; valuation-based shorts are early-and-wrong for months.
- **Defensive rotation:** Staples, utilities, low-vol underperform in relative terms; tilting defensive is a drag.
- **Mean-reversion fades on extended single names:** Momentum persists; fades get run over.
- **Cash-heavy positioning:** Sitting out the rally is the most expensive mistake in this regime.
- **Volatility-buying for tail-risk insurance:** Vol drains throughout the regime; long-vol structures bleed.

## Duration Expectations

The "early phase" of crisis recovery (sharpest rally, fastest spread compression, breadth thrust) typically runs 6-9 months from the panic low. The "extended phase" (continued grind higher, normalization toward balanced-expansion) runs another 12-24 months. The two historical examples diverge: 2009 transitioned slowly over years; 2020 transitioned in months because of the policy-response speed and shock idiosyncrasy.

Honest band: 6 months to 18+ months for the regime's full life. After that the system likely is in "balanced expansion" or a fresh regime entirely.

## Detection Signals

1. **Breadth thrust:** % of stocks above 50dma rises from <20% to >55% within 10-12 sessions, or the Zweig thrust criteria fire.
2. **Credit-spread inflection:** HY spreads peak and compress meaningfully (100+ bps tighter from peak).
3. **VIX term structure flips from backwardated to contango and stays there.**
4. **SPX recovers >15% from recent low without a >7% pullback.**
5. **Cyclical sectors (XLI, XLF, XLB) lead defensives on a rolling 60-day basis.**
6. **High-yield CDX tightens persistently for 4+ weeks.**

The combination is diagnostic; any one signal in isolation can fire in failed bounces.

## Exit Triggers

1. **A clean retest of the prior low that fails to hold.** The recovery thesis breaks; treat as a regime change back to crisis or a fresh regime.
2. **Credit spreads widening back to >75% of their crisis peak.** Risk appetite is reversing.
3. **Breadth deteriorating below 50% above 200dma while index makes a new high.** Recovery is morphing into late-cycle or topping.
4. **Policy regime change.** The central bank pivots from accommodative to tightening; the rally's liquidity foundation is removed.
5. **Cyclical leadership rolling over.** XLI/XLF underperform defensives for 8+ weeks.

The exit is often gradual rather than sharp. The regime morphs into "balanced expansion" via the same kind of leadership-rotation that ended its early phase.

## Confidence Notes

Crisis recovery is one of the better-documented regimes; the 2009 and 2020 cases are recent enough that data and primary sources are abundant. Both episodes share enough structural features that pattern-matching is on relatively solid ground.

Areas of lower confidence:
- The marker ranges are a blend of the 2009 and 2020 episodes, which differed materially in speed. A new crisis recovery may not look like either.
- The "what works" list is heavily influenced by the post-2009 era of massive policy accommodation. A crisis recovery without that policy response (e.g., one constrained by inflation, as 2022 partially demonstrated) may behave very differently.
- The exit triggers are oriented toward the "regime succeeds and transitions to expansion" case; the "recovery fails and we re-enter crisis" case is less well-fleshed-out.

This profile is a starting point. Refinement should track the next actual crisis recovery (if any) and improve handling of failed recoveries.
