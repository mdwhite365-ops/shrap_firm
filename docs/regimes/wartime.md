# Wartime

**Regime ID:** wartime
**Document version:** 0.1 (draft)
**Date:** 2026-05-29
**Owner:** Mike White

## Identification

A wartime regime is an environment in which a major conflict materially reshapes capital flows, supply chains, fiscal stance, and the sector mix of equity markets. Defense and aerospace contractors benefit from elevated procurement and supplemental appropriations; commodity-producing nations and producers benefit from supply disruption; logistics and energy infrastructure attract premium pricing; long-duration consumer-discretionary and globally-exposed names suffer. The regime is not the same as a brief geopolitical shock — those are events, not regimes. Wartime is a sustained reallocation, typically 18 months or longer, with a durable shift in the fiscal-spending mix.

This profile is informed by Mike's vision-document call for a defense-contractor sub-universe, but the regime tagging is about behavioral and structural shifts, not speculation about specific conflicts. The leverage Shrap aims for in this regime comes from reading primary sources — government contract awards (USASpending, SAM.gov), lobbying disclosures (Senate LDA filings), congressional appropriations text, supplemental budget requests — earlier and more carefully than the median market participant. It is not from "war drum" macro speculation.

## Historical Examples

- **1941-1945 (US WWII):** Massive fiscal expansion (defense spending peaked ~40% of GDP), rationing, capital controls. Defense and industrial production names benefited durably. Modern market microstructure differs so dramatically that this analog is more useful for fiscal-spending pattern than for direct market behavior.
- **1950-1953 (Korean War):** Defense spending expansion (~14% of GDP at peak), commodity disruption, inflation pulse. Defense contractors of the era outperformed broad market. Useful for the "limited-war but sustained-procurement" pattern.
- **2022 commodity spike (partial analog, post-Ukraine invasion):** Russian invasion of Ukraine (2022-02), energy and grain disruption, sanctions regime, sustained defense-spending increases across NATO. SPX drew down but commodity and defense names outperformed sharply. The "wartime tilt" was clear without the regime escalating to a global-war level. A useful sub-regime example: wartime-tilt without total mobilization.

## Macro Backdrop

Wartime regime characteristics:

- Sustained defense-spending increases relative to baseline, visible in supplemental appropriations and contract-award data.
- Supply-chain dislocation in commodities or strategic inputs (energy, semiconductors, rare earths, food, fertilizer).
- Elevated geopolitical risk premium in oil, gas, and select equities.
- Sanctions regimes that create persistent structural distortions.
- Government-procurement-driven revenue growth for selected industries (defense primes, ammunition, drones, logistics, energy infrastructure).
- Inflation pulse driven by supply constraints, often coexisting with strong nominal growth.
- Bipartisan defense-spending consensus that survives political cycles.

The regime is identified by the persistence of the structural change, not by the headline of a single conflict. A flare-up that ends in weeks is an event, not a regime. The wartime tag should fire when budget allocations, contract awards, and sustained supply distortion confirm a multi-quarter shift.

## What Works

- **Defense-prime exposure timed to contract-award cycles:** Long-tail data sources (USASpending obligations, SAM.gov solicitations, congressional appropriations text) front-run sell-side estimate revisions. Mike's Structural Analysis Department is built for this.
- **Energy and select commodity exposure:** Sustained supply disruption underpins term-structure backwardation; producers benefit.
- **Logistics, sealift, and supply-chain infrastructure equities:** Beneficiaries of procurement-driven volume.
- **Pair structures: long defense / short discretionary:** The dispersion is durable; pair structures harvest it cleanly.
- **Event-driven vol around appropriations cycles and escalation headlines:** Catalysts are calendared (supplemental budget votes, NDAA passage) and tradable.

## What Fails

- **Long globally-exposed consumer discretionary:** Supply costs, consumer-confidence shocks, and discretionary-spending compression bite.
- **Naive risk-parity allocation:** Bond-stock correlation is unstable; the diversification math breaks.
- **Short defense names on "valuation":** The backlog and award visibility extend further than valuation-only models recognize.
- **Long-duration tech at premium multiples:** Discount-rate environment is hostile and earnings-mix matters less.
- **Buy-the-dip on the index without sector discrimination:** The index hides huge sector dispersion; dip-buying must be sector-selective.

## Duration Expectations

Wartime regimes have historically run from roughly 3 years (Korean War) to multiple years across the WWII / Cold War boundary. The 2022 partial analog is still resolving as of this writing; the durability of the defense-spending shift has been multi-year already. Honest band: 18 months to 5+ years. Median expectation 3 years for a confirmed wartime regime, far less for the "tilt" sub-regime that does not fully escalate.

The regime can outlast the conflict that triggered it: procurement cycles, replenishment, and capability investment continue years after active hostilities end.

## Detection Signals

1. **Defense procurement obligations** (USASpending, monthly) rising >20% YoY for 6+ consecutive months.
2. **Supplemental appropriations bills** moving through Congress with bipartisan support.
3. **Defense-prime backlogs** (10-K and 10-Q reported) expanding faster than historical baseline.
4. **Commodity term-structure backwardation** in oil, grains, or strategic metals persisting >60 days.
5. **Sustained sector dispersion** with defense (ITA, XAR, PPA) and energy (XLE) outperforming SPY on rolling 60-day basis by >5%.
6. **Allied defense spending** (NATO-2% commitments, partner-nation budgets) trending higher with policy backing.
7. **Sanctions regimes** that show no near-term resolution path.

The structural-data signals (1, 2, 3, 6) are higher-confidence than the market-price signals (4, 5) because they are less subject to short-term sentiment.

## Exit Triggers

1. **Defense supplemental flow normalizes:** YoY procurement growth returns to single digits and stays there for two quarters.
2. **Backlog growth flattens at major primes:** The pipeline rolls over.
3. **Commodity term structure flips to durable contango** in previously-disrupted commodities.
4. **Sanctions regimes resolve** or are credibly lifted.
5. **Allied defense-spending consensus cracks.** Bipartisan support erodes; future appropriations face headwinds.
6. **Sector leadership rotates** away from defense and energy on a 60-day basis without re-establishing.

The exit is typically gradual and contested. False signals (a single quarter of weak procurement, a temporary commodity normalization) are common. The classifier should require multiple triggers and persistence before flipping the regime tag.

## Confidence Notes

The wartime profile is the most speculative of the four seed profiles. The WWII analog is structurally different from modern markets; the Korean War analog is dated; the 2022 partial analog is still resolving. Mike's vision-document framing is explicit: the leverage in this regime comes from primary-source reading (contract data, lobbying disclosures, appropriations text), not from macro speculation about conflicts.

Areas of explicit caution:
- The line between "wartime regime" and "elevated-geopolitical-tension event" is fuzzy in real time. Misclassifying an event as a regime would lead to overcommitting to a tilt that the market then unwinds.
- Defense-name behavioral characteristics have changed materially since the 1950s-1960s (program lifecycles, contracting structures, defense-budget mix). Historical analog is weaker for this sector than for, say, energy.
- The profile is biased toward US-centric defense-spending dynamics. A wartime regime triggered by a non-US-led conflict may behave differently.
- The Structural Analysis Department is core to making this regime profile tradable. Without disciplined primary-source reading, the wartime tag becomes a license to speculate, which is the opposite of the intended use.

This profile is a starting point. The Universe Curator and Structural Analysis Department should refine it as conflict episodes evolve. Mike's review is required before this regime is added to live routing; the speculative content demands explicit lock-in.
